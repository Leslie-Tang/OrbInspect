\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Large orbital structures such as space stations, service platforms, and
assembled space telescopes require periodic external inspection. Human
extravehicular activity is expensive and hazardous, while fixed cameras provide
limited coverage of complex external geometry. A small free-flying inspection
spacecraft can provide richer visual coverage, but planning its trajectory is a
coupled guidance, navigation, control, perception, and safety problem.

The difficulty is not merely producing a visually appealing simulation. A
research-grade inspection planner must answer the following questions: which
surface elements must be inspected, from which viewpoints can they be observed,
what trajectory connects these viewpoints under relative orbital dynamics, and
how safety margins are maintained near large structures. In addition, the
resulting experiment must produce reproducible trajectory, control, coverage,
safety, and planning logs suitable for academic evaluation.

This paper draft positions OrbInspect around the following contribution:
a coverage-constrained trajectory planner for large orbital structure inspection
under Clohessy--Wiltshire relative dynamics, with validation in ROS~2 and
Gazebo Harmonic. The present manuscript intentionally separates three layers:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item formal problem definition and mission assumptions;
  \item offline trajectory generation using a geometry-aware coverage model;
  \item ROS~2/Gazebo replay and validation using the generated trajectory.
\end{enumerate}

This separation avoids over-claiming from a short Gazebo visualization. Gazebo
is used to validate visualization, logging, and execution interfaces; the
coverage claim must come from the planner and its recorded metrics.
