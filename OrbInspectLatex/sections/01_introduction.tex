\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

Large orbital structures are becoming central to civil, scientific, and
commercial spaceflight. Space stations, assembled telescopes, logistics
platforms, and in-space manufacturing facilities all require periodic external
inspection to detect configuration changes, micrometeoroid damage, thermal
blanket degradation, docking-interface anomalies, and other faults that are
not easily observed from fixed onboard cameras. A small free-flying inspector
can provide close-range, target-specific imagery, but autonomous inspection in
the neighborhood of a large structure remains a coupled planning and validation
problem: the vehicle must acquire useful views, satisfy relative orbital
dynamics, remain outside keep-out regions, avoid excessive control effort, and
produce evidence that the mission actually inspected the requested targets.

This paper presents OrbInspect, a ROS~2-based framework for orbital inspection
planning, execution, and evaluation. The design is motivated by a practical
research need. Inspection algorithms are often demonstrated either as
stand-alone planners detached from middleware and visualization, or as
visually compelling simulators in which the quantitative coverage and safety
evidence is difficult to audit. OrbInspect separates those roles. A ROS-native
Clohessy--Wiltshire/Hill (CW/HCW) dynamics node is the source of truth for the
chaser state; controllers, safety filters, coverage estimators, mission logic,
and loggers are connected through ROS~2 interfaces; Gazebo Harmonic provides
visual validation of the station and chaser scene but is not used as the
orbital-dynamics authority.

The specific contribution of this manuscript is a coverage-constrained
inspection-planning and validation pipeline. We formulate the inspection task
in the local-vertical/local-horizontal frame, define target visibility and
dwell criteria, impose actuation and keep-out constraints, and implement a
mesh-aware offline planning experiment. The planner samples area-weighted mesh
targets, generates candidate viewpoints, computes visibility with range,
field-of-view, view-angle, and line-of-sight checks, scores viewpoints by new
coverage and CW transfer cost, exports camera attitudes, and produces a
bounded-acceleration CW reference trajectory. The resulting artifacts are stored in a
self-contained experiment directory containing raw CSV files, configuration
snapshots, figures, videos, and summary files.

The reported results show a substantial improvement over the earlier proxy
planner baseline. On the mesh benchmark, the proposed set-cover CW tour
achieves 98.33\% coverage of 180 target samples with all transfers feasible,
\unit{8.770}{m/s} cumulative $\Delta v$, and \unit{7.53}{m} minimum clearance.
The comparison includes six baselines: proposed safe CW next-best-view,
coverage-greedy, safe coverage-greedy, nearest next-best-view, fuel-greedy,
and random-safe. A ROS~2 paper-experiment smoke run verifies the execution and
logging pipeline over trajectory, control, coverage, planner, safety, and
rosbag topics. A Gazebo Harmonic validation path replays the saved offline
trajectory and attitude stream with an ISS visual model, confirming simulator
interfaces while leaving HCW dynamics as the state authority.

The main contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item a ROS~2 Jazzy architecture for orbital inspection in which HCW dynamics
  remain the state source of truth and Gazebo is used for visualization;
  \item a formal coverage-constrained relative trajectory planning problem
  combining visibility, dwell, fuel, speed, and clearance constraints;
  \item a mesh-aware dynamics-aware set-cover CW tour planner with saved
  target, viewpoint, attitude, trajectory, coverage, and planner logs;
  \item a self-contained validation package with figures, raw data, Gazebo
  frames, video artifacts, configuration snapshots, and summaries embedded
  under the paper directory;
  \item an evidence-based comparison showing 98.33\% coverage and lower
  $\Delta v$ than random-safe and several greedy baselines.
\end{enumerate}
