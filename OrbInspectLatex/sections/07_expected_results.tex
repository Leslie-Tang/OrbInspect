\subsection{Offline Planning Performance}

Table~\ref{tab:certified-results} reports the reduced-graph certification
case. The exact dynamic program used eight candidate nodes from a 16-candidate
mesh instance, covered 13 visible target IDs, expanded 39 feasible graph
states, and returned certificate status ``optimal''. The certified tour
selected two views, reached the required 25\% raw coverage, and remained
within the input and clearance limits. On the same reduced instance, the
scalable proposed solver and safe-coverage-greedy also selected two feasible
views but incurred higher HCW dynamic cost. The purpose of this case is not to
represent the full ISS mission; it demonstrates that the implemented finite
graph solver can return a genuine optimum when the reduced graph is small
enough to exhaust.

Table~\ref{tab:offline-results} summarizes the proposed dynamics-aware tour in
the high-coverage mesh experiment. The planner reached 98.33\% coverage over

planning stop target. Coverage is reported together with cumulative
$\Delta v$, minimum clearance, peak requested input, and clipping ratio because
these quantities determine whether an inspection sequence is dynamically
plausible. These results establish the scalable full-graph comparison only;
the sensitivity and ablation studies in Table~\ref{tab:validation-matrix} test
which parts of this ranking persist under denser target sampling and perturbed
mission conditions.

Figure~\ref{fig:validation-matrix-summary} summarizes the sensitivity and
ablation matrix. Increasing the target count from 180 to 500 preserves the
main ranking: the proposed tour remains feasible and reaches the 98\% stop
target with 21.38~m/s cumulative $\Delta v$, whereas safe-coverage-greedy
requires 47.08~m/s and coverage-greedy remains infeasible. The coarser
$N=1000$ proposed-only stress case remains feasible but reaches 76.5\%
coverage because the candidate stride is increased to keep mesh visibility
computation tractable; this identifies candidate-density coupling as a
remaining scalability issue rather than hiding it.

The initial-condition sweep over three safe starting states keeps the proposed
tour feasible with final coverage 98.33\%, cumulative $\Delta v$ between
20.19 and 23.09~m/s, and positive clearance. Candidate-density sweeps show
that reducing candidate count from 357 to 120 still reaches at least 97.2\%
coverage but reduces clearance to 0.73~m in the proposed tour. Transfer
duration is the clearest feasibility boundary: a 70~s segment requests
0.061~m/s$^2$, slightly above the 0.060~m/s$^2$ limit, and is therefore
audited as infeasible; 90~s and 120~s segments are feasible.

The ablation study provides direct evidence for the algorithmic components.
Removing transfer cost increases cumulative $\Delta v$ and peak input while
retaining coverage. Removing the input-feasibility check produces high
coverage but violates the input limit. Removing clearance filtering changes
the selected set and reduces final coverage to 90.0\% in this benchmark.
Unweighted coverage is nearly identical here because the sampled ISS targets
have similar effective weights, but it remains an explicit ablation because
area weighting becomes important for nonuniform meshes.

\subsection{Camera Geometry and Replay Verification}

The high-coverage experiment exports trajectory and camera-attitude streams
for every compared method. For the proposed tour, each stream contains 945
samples. The camera orientation data replay the planned path with the same
boresight alignment used in the visibility predicate. The trajectory replay
node uses these data to publish odometry, path markers, field-of-view markers,
and station mesh markers for RViz and Gazebo validation.

Figures~\ref{fig:camera-model-frusta} and~\ref{fig:camera-model-coverage}
make the camera model visible in two ways. The first reconstructs
representative field-of-view frusta from selected view poses and boresight
vectors. The second reports the incremental surface coverage contributed by
the selected-view sequence. Together, they show that saved attitudes
instantiate the same FOV geometry used by the visibility predicate and that
the selected views contribute to the final coverage.

planner topics. This verifies that the visual model, attitude stream, and
geometric visibility logic remain consistent after integration.
