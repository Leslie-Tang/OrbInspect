\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion}

The main result is that coverage and orbital feasibility cannot be separated
without changing the mission outcome. In the reported benchmark, the
proposed dynamics-aware tour reaches 98.33\% coverage with positive
clearance, the lowest $\Delta v$ among feasible methods that reach the
high-coverage stop target, and a comparatively low peak requested input. The
coverage-greedy method reaches the same coverage but requires acceleration
beyond the admissible input limit, while nearest and random-safe selection
also become infeasible because of clearance or input violations. Coverage
alone is therefore not an adequate objective for orbital inspection.

The sensitivity matrix strengthens this conclusion but also shows its
boundary. The proposed planner remains feasible under the $N=500$ target
density, three tested initial states, candidate strides from 1 to 3, and
keep-out margins from 1 to 4~m. However, the coarser $N=1000$ stress case
falls below the 98\% stop target because candidate density was reduced for
runtime, and the 70~s transfer-duration case violates the input limit. Thus,
the method is not simply ``robust'' in an unqualified sense. It is robust over
the completed moderate sweeps, but high-density mesh coverage requires better
candidate generation, visibility acceleration, or a larger view budget.

The benchmark still uses a downsampled line-of-sight representation for
occlusion checking and a CW/HCW translational model. These choices are
appropriate for a reproducible algorithmic baseline, but the validation
matrix shows that dense target sets quickly make line-of-sight and transfer
evaluation expensive. A flight-oriented planner would require accelerated
mesh queries, uncertainty-inflated keep-out volumes, camera slew limits,
illumination constraints, state-estimation error, plume constraints, and
actuator saturation studies. The reported RMS transfer tracking error is also
a planner-side rollout metric rather than a closed-loop flight validation
error.

The optimality statement is similarly bounded. The implemented dynamic program
certifies the minimum-cost sequence only on the fixed reduced candidate graph
whose reachable states are fully expanded. The reported high-coverage ISS
experiment uses a scalable deterministic solver on the same graph model, so
its evidence is empirical rather than a global certificate for the full graph
or for the original continuous viewpoint space. This split is intentional: the
reduced case verifies the optimization statement, whereas the full benchmark
tests whether the same dynamics-aware modeling choices remain useful at ISS
mesh scale.

Gazebo validation should be interpreted conservatively. The videos and frames
verify that the visual model, chaser model, camera view, and pose-following
interfaces work in the integrated stack. They do not validate orbital
mechanics, prove coverage, or certify collision avoidance. Those quantitative
claims come from the HCW dynamics, coverage, trajectory, attitude, safety, and
planner logs.
