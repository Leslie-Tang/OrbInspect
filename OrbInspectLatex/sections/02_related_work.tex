\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Orbital inspection draws on relative spacecraft motion, robotic view planning,
and orbit-design methods for complex structures. The Clohessy--Wiltshire
equations remain a common starting point for rendezvous and proximity
operations near a circular chief orbit because they provide a linear,
interpretable model of relative translation \cite{clohessy1960terminal}.
Although high-fidelity perturbation models are required for flight design, a
CW/HCW model is appropriate for the planning question studied here: how view
ordering affects fuel use, clearance, and input demand under a fixed relative
dynamics model \cite{wertz2011space}.

Robotic inspection and next-best-view planning study how a robot should select
sensor poses that reconstruct or inspect an object efficiently. Classical
surveys emphasize visibility, occlusion, sensor range, view quality, and the
trade-off between coverage and motion cost \cite{scott2003view}. Kinodynamic
sampling-based planners, including rapidly exploring random trees and their
optimal variants, explicitly combine dynamics and collision constraints during
motion planning \cite{lavalle1998rapidly,karaman2011sampling}. Their strength
is continuous-state reachability, whereas coverage is usually encoded through
task-specific rewards, terminal goals, or repeated replanning rather than as a
finite weighted dwell-coverage objective over a spacecraft surface.

Recent spacecraft-inspection studies illustrate the same tension from
different directions. Apodaca et al. couple viewpoint planning with trajectory
generation for in-orbit structure inspection and emphasize coverage-energy
trade-offs in a supervised planning pipeline \cite{apodaca2025trajectory}.
Hou et al. design relative orbits for visual inspection of the China Space
Station and analyze field-of-view and occlusion feasibility through orbital
geometry \cite{hou2024relative}. Zhou et al. plan tomographic inspection paths
around complex spacecraft structures with surface-aware obstacle avoidance
\cite{zhou2026tomographic}. Ogri et al. address a complementary online
problem: image-based feature tracking, information gain, and dwell-time
conditions for autonomous inspection under switched systems
\cite{ogri2025switched}. The present work is positioned between these lines:
it does not introduce a new orbital dynamics model, a flight-certified
collision-avoidance controller, or a globally optimal continuous planner.
Its contribution is the offline finite candidate-graph problem and solver in
which surface coverage, camera feasibility, HCW transfer effort, clearance,
and input saturation are evaluated before the inspection tour is accepted.

Table~\ref{tab:positioning} clarifies what is gained and lost by the finite
candidate-view graph. The gain is a common discrete object on which coverage,
camera feasibility, edge feasibility, clearance, input limits, and exact
finite-graph certification can be stated simultaneously. The cost is that
optimality is conditional on the sampled candidate set and precomputed
feasible-transfer graph; the approach does not provide global optimality in the
continuous viewpoint space, nor does it replace kinodynamic planning when a
mission requires continuous online obstacle avoidance. The safety statement is
therefore planning-level feasibility filtering on logged HCW rollouts, not a
flight safety certificate.

Simulation frameworks for space robotics must also be treated carefully. A
rendered Gazebo scene can provide visual context and reveal integration errors
in camera geometry, but standard rigid-body physics in a robotics simulator is
not a substitute for orbital relative dynamics. We therefore use Gazebo
Harmonic for station and chaser visualization while the ROS~2 dynamics node
publishes the state used by replay, safety checks, camera-frustum
verification, and logging. This separation makes the validation repeatable
without allowing simulator physics to define the scientific result.
