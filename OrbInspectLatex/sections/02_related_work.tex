\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

Orbital inspection combines ideas from spacecraft relative motion, visual
inspection planning, next-best-view planning, and safe control. Relative motion
near a circular chief orbit is commonly modeled with the
Clohessy--Wiltshire/Hill equations, which provide a tractable baseline for
guidance design and trajectory optimization \citep{clohessy1960terminal}.
Higher-fidelity models can later
be incorporated through numerical astrodynamics backends, but a linear
relative-motion baseline is valuable for reproducible algorithm development.

Coverage planning and next-best-view methods are widely used in robotics for
inspection of complex geometry \citep{scott2003view}. However, orbital inspection differs from
terrestrial inspection because viewpoints are constrained by orbital dynamics,
thruster authority, keep-out zones, line-of-sight blockage, and fuel. The
planner must therefore optimize over both sensing utility and dynamic
feasibility.

OrbInspect is designed to bridge this gap by combining a coverage model, a
relative-dynamics trajectory generator, safety constraints, and ROS~2-based
experiment logging. The NASA International Space Station mesh is used for
visual realism, while simplified primitives are retained initially for
occlusion and safety certificates until mesh-accelerated ray casting is
implemented.
