\begin{abstract}
Autonomous inspection of large orbital structures requires view plans that are simultaneously informative, dynamically reachable, and safe under relative orbital motion. This paper formulates the offline inspection problem
as a finite candidate-view graph whose nodes encode camera-feasible viewpoints and whose directed edges encode bounded-acceleration Clohessy--Wiltshire/Hill transfers, clearance, and input feasibility. The resulting planner couples area-weighted coverage gain with orbital transfer cost during view selection, instead of selecting geometric views first and checking dynamics afterward. For reduced graphs, an exact dynamic program over selected-view masks and terminal-view nodes provides a discrete optimality certificate. For full ISS-mesh instances, a scalable deterministic solver uses the same visibility and transfer model to generate high-coverage tours. The method is evaluated on an open NASA ISS mesh against coverage, nearest-view, fuel-focused, CW-NBV, random-safe, and safety-filtered coverage baselines. The results show that geometry-only high-coverage planners can violate input or clearance constraints, whereas the proposed dynamics-aware planner reaches the high-coverage stop target with lower energy than feasible safe baselines. Sensitivity and ablation studies further identify the effects of target
density, candidate density, initial condition, safety margin, transfer duration, transfer cost, clearance filtering, and input checking. ROS~2 and Gazebo replay verify that the logged trajectory and camera field-of-view
geometry are consistent with the offline planner, while the quantitative claims remain based on the HCW dynamics and planner logs.
\end{abstract}
